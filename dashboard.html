How these numbers are calculated

No water meter readings exist publicly for these sites, so every number on this page is an estimate based on each facility's reported power use, not a direct measurement.

The formula

Water per year = Power use (MW) × 12.78 × load factor

The 12.78 figure comes from running a 1-megawatt facility for a year at an industry-average rate of 1.8 liters of water per kilowatt-hour. A 100-megawatt facility running at full capacity would use roughly enough water to supply a small town for a year.

2050 projection

This applies the 2017–2027 growth rate forward another 23 years. Over a horizon that long, small changes in the assumed rate produce very different results, which is why the slider above lets you adjust it. These are sensitivity scenarios, not forecasts.

What this screening doesn't include yet

Not yet incorporated: groundwater level readings, spring flow records, and satellite measurements of underground water storage. The recharge zone boundary used in the map and in the calculation above is the official TCEQ regulatory boundary (City of Austin GIS mirror of the TCEQ Edwards Aquifer Protection Program map, 30 TAC 213), not an approximation. The remaining limitation is facility location precision, described above.

Known issues with the data

Of the 28 data centers in the original 7-county screening: 14 have public information on power use, 9 don't disclose it, 4 could not be confirmed as real or distinct facilities (possible duplicates or mislabeled entries), and 1 (Samsung's chip-manufacturing plant in Austin) is not a data center and is excluded from all totals.

Williamson County addition

Williamson is one of 8 counties TCEQ designates as part of the Edwards Aquifer Protection Program area (30 TAC 213), but it was not included in the original 7-county report. We added it here using local news reporting (Austin Free Press, Hello Georgetown) rather than the comptroller registry used for the other 7 counties, since the registry alone didn't capture Williamson's scale. 11 facilities are listed; only 3 have a publicly reported capacity figure (685 MW combined). County-wide reporting suggests roughly 8 facilities are currently operating with about 500 MW combined, and around 30 are operating or planned in total, both well in excess of what we can name and locate individually here.

We checked all 39 facilities, including Williamson, against the official TCEQ recharge zone boundary map. 3 Williamson facilities fall within a regulated zone (1 Recharge Zone, 2 Transition Zone); the rest, including several large planned campuses in Hutto and Taylor, do not. As noted above, this check uses estimated city-center coordinates rather than verified street addresses, so it should be read as a starting point, not a final determination, for any individual facility.

Groundwater Conservation Districts and major rivers

The map also includes Groundwater Conservation District (GCD) boundaries, sourced from TWDB's official statewide GCD map (current as of November 2019), and major rivers (TWDB, 2010), both toggleable from the legend. GCDs are the local entities with legal authority to regulate how much groundwater can be pumped in their area; outside a GCD boundary, groundwater pumping in Texas is governed by the "rule of capture," which gives landowners broad rights to pump groundwater under their property with few limits. Checking our 39 facilities against this map found that 19 sit outside any GCD boundary at all. The same city-center coordinate caveat applies here as it does for the aquifer zone check above.
