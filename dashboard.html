How depletion numbers are calculated

No water meter readings exist publicly for these sites, so every number here is an estimate based on each facility's reported power use, not a direct measurement.

Why this map only covers 8 counties, and a different 8 than the pollution map

This map is scoped to the 8 counties the Edwards Aquifer Authority (EAA) has jurisdiction over: Bexar, Medina, Uvalde, Atascosa, Caldwell, Comal, Guadalupe, and Hays. That's a different list from the pollution map's 8 TCEQ counties. Travis, Kinney, and Williamson have no EAA jurisdiction, so facilities there (19 of them) are excluded from this map even though they appear on the pollution map. Atascosa has no confirmed, locatable data center project as of this research.

The formula

Water per year = Power use (MW) × 12.78 × load factor

The 12.78 figure comes from running a 1-megawatt facility for a year at an industry-average rate of 1.8 liters of water per kilowatt-hour.

Most facilities don't pump the aquifer directly

Most data centers in this region draw from municipal water systems (e.g. San Antonio Water System) rather than operating their own industrial well, even when located within EAA jurisdiction. SAWS itself operates 92 wells pumping about 204 million gallons a day to serve its customers. A data center's water use adds demand onto that municipal system, which in turn pumps the aquifer on its behalf, an indirect but real link, not a direct private withdrawal in most cases.

Groundwater Conservation Districts

GCDs are the local entities with legal authority to regulate how much groundwater can be pumped in their area. Outside a GCD boundary, Texas groundwater is governed by the "rule of capture," giving landowners broad rights to pump with few limits. Source: TWDB official statewide GCD map, current as of November 2019.

Guadalupe and Caldwell County addition

The Edwards Aquifer Authority has jurisdiction over portions of Guadalupe and Caldwell counties, which TCEQ's Edwards Aquifer Protection Program does not cover. We added 3 facilities here: CloudBurst's 1.2 GW campus (Guadalupe/Hays line, with a developer-disclosed 24,000 gal/day water use figure), and Tract's and Prime Data Centers' campuses near Lockhart/Uhland in Caldwell County. Checking these against the EAA's own zone map found that none of the three sit within EAA's mapped Artesian, Recharge, or Contributing zones, their coordinates fall just east of where EAA's mapped boundary ends, even though the counties themselves are listed as partial EAA jurisdiction. All three do fall within the Plum Creek Groundwater Conservation District. Atascosa County is not included: no confirmed, locatable project exists as of this research.

Known issues with the data

Of the 28 data centers in the original 7-county screening: 14 have public power-use information, 9 don't disclose it, 4 could not be confirmed as real or distinct facilities, and 1 (Samsung's Austin fab) is not a data center and is excluded. Williamson County's 11 facilities were added from local news reporting rather than the comptroller registry.

How pollution potential is calculated

This estimates how much stormwater runoff a facility's developed site could generate during a single rainfall event, using established EPA-recognized methodology, not site-specific engineering data. It is a comparative volume estimate, not a measurement of actual pollution.

Step 1: Power capacity to site size

Acres = MW ÷ 3.5

Source: Hines Research, "every three to four megawatts of power capacity requires an acre of land."

Step 2: Site size to impervious surface

Impervious acres = Site acres × impervious fraction (adjustable, 60-80%)

Source: JPC Engineering civil infrastructure analysis, "the combined impervious fraction on a developed data center campus can exceed 70 to 80 percent of total site area" (buildings, parking, access roads, substation yards, equipment pads). The slider above defaults to 75%, the midpoint, and lets you test the full cited range; for comparison, typical land-use studies classify commercial development at ~85% impervious and industrial at ~75%.

Step 3: Impervious surface to runoff volume

Rv = 0.05 + 0.9 × I   Runoff = Rv × Rainfall(1") × Acres ÷ 12

This is the EPA-recognized "Simple Method" (Schueler, 1987), used in state stormwater design manuals nationwide, applied here with Texas's standard 1-inch design storm depth.

Why this matters in karst terrain

The Edwards Aquifer is a karst aquifer: fractures, sinkholes, and caves can carry surface runoff directly underground with minimal natural filtration. Roughly 15% of the continental US has karst topography, and it is recognized as especially vulnerable to stormwater contaminant infiltration. A facility's runoff volume matters most where it sits on or near a TCEQ-regulated zone.

A second pathway: cooling-tower blowdown

Stormwater runoff is about contamination that washes in from outside. Blowdown is different: it's wastewater the facility itself routinely discharges. Evaporative cooling systems concentrate dissolved minerals and added chemicals (biocides to control algae and mold, corrosion inhibitors, and trace heavy metals like copper and zinc from degrading components) in the water that doesn't evaporate. To prevent scale buildup, a portion of that concentrated water has to be periodically drained, this is "blowdown."

Blowdown per year = Total water use ÷ Cycles of Concentration

Cycles of concentration is an industry setting typically run between 4 and 6; lower cycles mean more frequent blowdown. We use 4 (the more conservative, higher-blowdown end of that range) applied to the same total water-use figure as the depletion map. This is a derived estimate from documented industry ratios, not a site-specific measurement, no facility here publicly discloses its actual blowdown volume or chemical treatment program.

What this is not

Neither runoff nor blowdown measures or assumes the presence of any specific pollutant. Both are volume estimates based on industry-average figures applied uniformly to every facility, not real site plans or discharge permits. Treat them as relative comparisons, not engineering-grade figures.
